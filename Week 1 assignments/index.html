Introduction

Carrom Board is one of the popular indoor games like ludo. It is a tabletop game of Indian origin in which players flick discs, attempting to knock them to the corners of the board. In South Asia, many clubs and cafés hold regular tournaments. Carrom is commonly played by families, including children, and at social functions.This game is an awesome example of sheer skills, immaculate accuracy, and an absolute thriller. Different standards and rules exist in different areas.



History

The history of carrom origins can be traced back to the 18th century in the Indian subcontinent. While concrete evidence is scarce, it’s widely believed that the game first took shape in the palaces of Indian Maharajas. In fact, a carrom board with a glass surface, dating back to that era, is still preserved in a Patiala palace.

Another theory suggests that the game of Carrom may have its origins in Portugal and gained popularity during their 19th-century influence in India and Asia. There’s also an interesting idea that carrom origin goes back even further. Similar boards to those used in Carrom have been found in Ancient Egypt, and there are mentions of a game resembling Carrom in ancient Greek literature.

As for its name, “carrom” is thought to have come from Timor in Southeast Asia and made its way to India through Portuguese connections. Carrom is also spelled as कैरम, Carom, Carum, Karom, and Kairam. According to the Cambridge dictionary, “carrom” means ‘to hit something and bounce back, especially at an angle.’

True to its name, Carrom focuses on striking disc-shaped pieces called carrommen with a striker. With the advent of modern manufacturing, Carrom boards evolved to use materials like plywood and plastic. While the equipment has changed, the strategic game of Carrom remains unchanged from its ancient Carrom origins.

That being said, today, Carrom transcends its status as merely a game or a pastime. It has transformed into a beloved tabletop sport, captivating the affection of millions worldwide and finding representation in national and international competitions.



Game Rules

  • Carrom is a strategy game for 2 to 4 players. In 2 player games, opponents sit opposite of each other, and for 4 players, partners are used and sit opposite of each other. The only difference between these two game plays is the use of partners and seating, all gameplay is the same. In a three-player game, you play for points.
  • The goal of the game is to score points by being the first player to clear the boar once the queen has been successfully pocketed. The goal is to reach 25 points, but if this does not occur before 8 boards are played the winner is the player with the highest point total. Below is a diagram with the needed layout for the opening board and the terminology needed for the game.
  • The first player is chosen randomly. They will be the white player in 2 and 4 player games. The board should be set up so that the queen is in the center and is surrounded by 6 pieces of alternating black and white, in the next bigger circle there should be 12 pieces of alternating black and white. Unlike the diagram above, you want double white instead of black and you want them to line up with the net holes as closely as possible. Once the board is set the first player will place their striker and will have 3 chances to break the center circle.
  • When placing a striker, a player must place it between the two parallel baselines. They may also place it completely on the red bases at the end of the baselines but may not place them partially on the base and baselines. When striking you may not have your arms, hands or legs cross the diagonal foul likes at the corners of the board. You must also only flick with your finger and not push, and the finer used must cross the front baseline when flicking.
  • For three-player games, the goal is to score the most points, up to 25 to win, and the most if 8 game boards are reached. There are no pieces assigned to players, instead, points are assigned to pieces. The black pieces are worth 1 point, whites are worth 2 points and the queen is worth 5 points.
  • On a player’s turn, they will use their striker to try and pocket their pieces. If they are successful in pocketing one of their pieces or the queen, they receive their striker back and strike again. This continues until no pieces are pocketed.Once no pieces or pocketed or a foul is committed that player’s turn ends and the next player may start theirs.
  • The queen is a special piece. It can only be pocketed once you have pocketed a piece of your own and if you do pocket it you must pocket another of your pieces to “cover” it. If the queen is pocketed before you pocket another piece it is returned to the center of the board at the end of the turn. If not covered the opponent may place the queen as close to the center as possible at the end of the turn.
  • Fouls end a player’s turn immediately and a penalty is applied to the player who committed it. Penalties are having a pocketed piece and any other pieces that need to be returned placed in the circle by an opponent.
  • Fouls can be a lot of things. Fouls include: pocketing a striker, having any pieces leave the board, pocketing an opponent’s pieces (in this case the opponent’s piece and the queen if relevant are also returned, other pieces are left pocketed, besides the penalty piece), pocketing all your pieces before the queen is pocketed (both pocketed and the penalty piece are retuned), pocketing the opponent’s last piece (it is returned along with a penalty piece), a player doesn’t break the center in the first three attempts, a player touches a piece on the board other than the striker, and if you do not follow the rules of striking.
  • When returning pieces, they may be staked on others. A piece is always left how it rest even if overlapping another or on its side. If the striker is caught under another piece it can be removed but the other piece must be disturbed as little as possible.
  • After the queen has been successfully pocketed any player may pocket their last piece to end the board. This is the winner of the board. The winner scores a point for each piece of their opponent’s not pocketed. If the winner was also the player to pocket the queen, they score an additional 5 points; Otherwise, the queen is not scored.
  • The game ends if a player reaches 25 or more points and they are the winner. The game also ends if 8 boards are completed. The player with the highest score wins.

How to Get Started

To get started with Carrom, you need a Carrom board, 18 carrom men pieces(9 black and 9 white), a queen, and a striker. The goal of this game is to pocket all of one's carrom men (playing pieces) before the opponent does.

  • Equipment:Carrom board, 18 carrom men pieces(9 black and 9 white), a queen, and a striker.
  • Basic Strategy: To win a game of carrom, players must strategize and aim carefully to pocket their carrom men and the queen before their opponent (s) do.
  • Beginner Tips: Here are some tips and tricks for you to get started with carrom.
    • Master Your Grip and Stance:
    A good striker grip is essential. Maintain a comfortable yet firm grip on the striker with your thumb and forefinger. Your stance is also crucial; keep your feet shoulder-width apart to ensure stability and balance. This is the foundation for accurate shots.
    Improve Your Striking Technique:
    Instead of relying solely on power, concentrate on the control and finesse of your strikes. Strike the carrom men with the striker's sweet spot to ensure a clean and accurate shot. This is especially important when trying to pocket carrom men in specific locations on the board.
    Understand Angles and Rebounds:
    Take the time to learn how carrom men react to various angles and rebounds on the court. Recognise how striking a carrom man at a specific angle can alter its trajectory, allowing you to plan better and execute shots.
    Check out the Board Dynamics:
    Take note of the condition of the board, taking into account factors such as the amount of powder on the surface and the arrangement of carrom men. Successful gameplay must adapt your strategy based on the board's dynamics.
    Carrommen's Strategic Positioning:
    Plan your moves by positioning your carrom men strategically on the board. This includes both offensive moves, such as placing carrom men for easy pockets, and defensive moves, such as positioning carrom men in positions that make it difficult for your opponent to score.
    Defensive Practise:
    Learn defensive techniques to keep your carrom men from being pocketed. This includes blocking your opponent's path to specific pockets, creating obstacles on the board, and defensively positioning your carrom men.
    Stroking Consistency:
    Maintain a consistent stroke. Repeatedly practise your shots to develop muscle memory and ensure that your strikes are constant in force and direction. Consistency is essential for increasing accuracy and overall performance.
    Adapt to Different Board Conditions:
    The smoothness and speed of carrom boards can vary. Practise on various boards to adapt your gameplay to different situations. This adaptability will make you a more versatile player who can perform well on a variety of surfaces.
    Study Professional Matches:
    Check out professional Carrom matches to learn about advanced techniques and strategies used by seasoned players. Please take note of their positioning, shot selection, and defensive strategies. Professional gameplay analysis can provide valuable insights that you can incorporate into your strategy.
    Persistence and patience:
    Carrom is a game that rewards perseverance and patience. Understand that progress takes time and that staying calm under pressure is essential. Consistent practice, combined with a patient attitude, will help you grow as a carrom player significantly.
